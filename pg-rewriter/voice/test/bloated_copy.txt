It could reasonably be suggested that, when one endeavours to cultivate proficiency within the broad domain of creative work, the most efficacious methodology is arguably not the one that is most frequently advocated. In many respects, individuals seeking to develop their capabilities are routinely encouraged to model their efforts upon those works that have been institutionally validated or that carry a certain measure of cultural prestige. However, it may be posited that a considerably more productive approach involves the deliberate emulation of those things which one genuinely and authentically appreciates, irrespective of their perceived status. To a certain extent, the works one is told ought to be admired tend to facilitate a somewhat hollow form of imitation, whereas the leveraging of one's own sincere preferences would appear, on balance, to engender a more meaningful and ultimately more generative engagement with the creative process as it is broadly understood.
