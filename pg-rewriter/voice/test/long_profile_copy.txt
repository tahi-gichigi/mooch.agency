In high school I spent a lot of time imitating bad writers. The fiction we read in English class led me to assume that fiction was the highest form of writing. The stories that seemed most admired were ones where characters suffered in complicated ways. Anything funny, or gripping, was suspect — unless it was old enough, like Shakespeare. The ideal form seemed to be the short story, which flourished briefly at the peak of magazine publishing. Because these pieces were so short, nothing actually had to happen. You could slice off a random cross-section of experience and that counted as sophisticated. So I wrote a lot of stories where nothing occurred except that someone was unhappy in a way that looked profound.

For most of college I studied philosophy. I was impressed by the papers in philosophy journals, though I never quite understood them. I tried to imitate them anyway. That was doomed from the start, because they weren't actually saying anything. No philosopher ever refutes another, because no one makes a claim specific enough to refute. My imitations didn't say anything either.
