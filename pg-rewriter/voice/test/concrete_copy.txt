It is worth observing that during my high school years I devoted a considerable amount of time to the imitation of writers who were, in retrospect, not very good. The curriculum in our English classes consisted predominantly of fiction, which led me to operate under the assumption that fiction represented the highest and most prestigious form of writing one could aspire to. This was, it must be said, the first of several errors. The narratives that appeared to command the greatest admiration were those in which characters underwent suffering of an elaborate and complicated nature. Anything that happened to be funny or genuinely gripping was, more or less automatically, regarded with suspicion, unless of course it was sufficiently old as to be difficult to comprehend, in the manner of Shakespeare or Chaucer. That constituted the second error. The short story was widely held to be the ideal medium, despite the fact that, as I subsequently came to understand, it had enjoyed a relatively brief period of prominence that coincided roughly with the peak of magazine publishing.
