In high school I spent a lot of time copying writers who, I later realized, weren't very good.

Part of the problem was what we read in English class. It was mostly fiction, so I assumed fiction was the highest kind of writing. That was the first mistake.

The stories that got the most respect were the ones where people suffered in complex ways. If something was funny, or made you want to keep reading, that counted against it. Unless it was so old that it was hard to understand, like Shakespeare or Chaucer. That was the second mistake.

And everyone seemed to treat the short story as the perfect form. Only later did I realize that the short story's glory days were not some eternal law of art. They were tied to a fairly brief moment when magazines mattered a lot.
