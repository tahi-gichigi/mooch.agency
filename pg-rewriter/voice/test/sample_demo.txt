It is frequently asserted that the most effective way to learn to cook is to begin with elaborate recipes drawn from celebrated chefs and Michelin-starred restaurants. Aspiring cooks are routinely encouraged to attempt the soufflés and the intricate multi-component plates that are widely understood to signal seriousness and culinary prestige. It may, however, be considerably more productive to commence with the dish that one genuinely craves on an ordinary Tuesday evening: the simple bowl of pasta, or the fried egg on buttered toast. The ambitious recipe tends, in many respects, to teach one primarily to follow instructions. The humble thing one actually loves, by contrast, teaches one to taste, to adjust the seasoning, to notice the precise moment at which the egg is done. Skill, it would seem, develops a good deal faster when one genuinely cares about the result.
